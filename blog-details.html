Mastering the Wet-on-Wet Technique

Sarah Jenkins
Feb 10, 2026 • 5 min read

Watercolor is unique among mediums because its primary agent is water. While this makes it unpredictable, it also allows for effects impossible to achieve with oil or acrylic.

1. The Preparation

Before you even touch brush to paper, you must ensure your surface is ready. Use heavyweight cotton paper, preferably cold press, which has a slight texture that grabs the pigment. Wet the entire surface with clean water using a large flat brush or sponge. The paper should be damp and glistening, but not swimming in a puddle.

2. Dropping the Color

Load your brush with pigment. Touch the tip to the wet paper. Watch as the color explodes outward in soft, feathery blooms. This is the magic of wet-on-wet. You are not painting a shape; you are inviting the color to move.

"Control the water, and you control the painting. Or rather, you learn to dance with it."

3. Timing is Everything

If the paper dries too much, you will get hard edges. If it's too wet, the color will dilute into nothing. Learning this timing takes practice. It is a feeling, a rhythm that you develop over time.
